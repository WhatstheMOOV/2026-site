New Laws Are Mandating Student IDs. Here's How to Make Them Actually Useful.

MMOOV Team4 min read
A MOOV student ID card tapped at a building entrance

More states are passing laws about student ID cards every year. A growing list, including California, Texas, South Carolina, Louisiana, and Illinois, now requires public and private schools to print crisis and suicide prevention hotline numbers, like the 988 Lifeline, on student IDs for grades 7 through 12. On top of that, more districts are requiring students to wear IDs for building access and safety.

So schools are handing out more IDs than ever. But here's the problem every administrator already knows: a student ID only helps if the student actually has it on them. If the card doesn't do anything, it ends up at the back of their home desk drawer or lost by October.

The fix isn't stricter rules about carrying the ID. It's making the ID worth carrying.

An ID kids lose vs. an ID kids use

Think about the difference between a card a student needs once and a card a student uses all day.

If the only job of an ID is to be checked at the door, students treat it like a hall pass they can live without. They forget it, share it, or lose it, and now you're printing replacements and running temporary IDs all year.

But when the ID is the key to the whole building, students keep track of it the same way they keep track of their phone. That's the model MOOV is built on.

What a useful student ID looks like

With MOOV, the student ID card is a working tool from the first bell to the last. Students tap the same card to:

  • Enter the building in the morning
  • Take their own attendance when they walk into class
  • Get and return hall passes
  • Tap in at the nurse, guidance, the library, and the cafeteria
  • Earn reward points for tapping in to class early or on time!

When one card does all of that, forgetting it isn't a small inconvenience, it's the thing standing between a student and their whole day. So they bring it. Schools using MOOV consistently see students carrying and even personalizing their IDs, turning a compliance headache into something students actually own.

It also means your ID program does double duty. The card satisfies the legal requirements, like the printed crisis hotline number, and it powers attendance, safety, and accountability across the building. One card, one system, one thing for students to remember.

Frequently asked questions

Which states require student ID cards to include a suicide prevention hotline?

Multiple states, including California, Texas, South Carolina, Louisiana, Illinois, Arizona, Washington, and New Jersey, now require crisis or suicide prevention hotline information, such as the 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline, on student IDs for grades 7 through 12. The exact requirements vary by state, so check your state's specific law.

Are schools required to give students ID cards?

It depends on the state and district. Many states regulate what must be printed on an ID card if a school issues one, and a growing number of districts now require students to carry or wear IDs for building security. Requirements are expanding, so it's worth confirming your local rules.

How do you get students to keep their ID cards?

Make the card useful. When a student ID is needed to enter the building, take attendance, and get a hall pass throughout the day, students are far more likely to keep it with them than when the card only gets checked occasionally.

Can one student ID handle both the legal requirements and daily school operations?

Yes. With a system like MOOV, the same card can carry the required printed information and also power attendance, entry, hall passes, and safety, so schools don't need separate cards or systems.
